CSE 121a: Week 03

Ponder and Prove

The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) of integers is well known in math. This method for finding, usually for a pair of numbers, which of their divisors that they have in common is the greatest dates back to Euclid, and is usually studied early on in a course on algorithms.

More precisely, the GCD of two nonnegative, not-both-zero integers a and b, denoted gcd(a, b), is defined as the largest integer that divides both a and b evenly, i.e., with a remainder of zero. As you will learn when you take a course on discrete mathematics, the central fact of numbers exploited by the algorithm that computes the GCD is that

gcd(a, b) = gcd(b, a % b),

where ‘%’ is the mod operator that returns the remainder when a is divided by b.

Here is high-level pseudocode for the GCD algorithm:

1. Apply repeatedly the equality

   gcd(a, b) = gcd(b, a % b)

2. until a % b is equal to 0.

Since gcd(n, 0) = n, for any n, the last b (which is also the last non-zero remainder) is the answer — the GCD of the original a and b.

One of the main uses of the GCD function is to convert fractions to lowest terms. This is done by finding the GCD of the numerator and denominator of the fraction, and dividing both by their GCD. It is helpful to trace through this function to get an idea of how it works.
