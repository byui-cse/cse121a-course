Ponder and Prove

Introduction

Run-Length Encoding (RLE) is an interesting type of compression for data files. Some files, like bitmap images, have large areas where the numbers representing the image don’t change (imagine an image with large numbers of big black areas in it). The idea behind RLE is this: if there are 100 fives in a row, why keep all the fives? Instead, let us replace 99 of those fives with a 100. Then the pair 100 5 can represent the 100 fives in a lot less disk space. There are many different RLE algorithms, and you should feel free to explore those used to compress files, but your task is more interesting than that. That’s because you are going to compress a subset of an infinite set!

The set of Natural numbers is infinite. Here is a mathmatical representation of an RLE-encoded finite sequence of numbers beginning at 2 using a unique and interesting version of Run-Length Encoding:

RLEPRIMES=(1,1,0,1,0,1,3,1,0,1,3,1,0,1,3,1,5,1,0,1,5,1,3,1,0,1,3,1,5,1,5,1,0,1,5,1,3,1,0,1,5,1,3,1,5,1,7,1,3)

The RLE algorithm used to produce this set of numbers is different than that used for images. In it, a 1 represents a prime number, a 0 represents an isolated composite number, and any other number found in the sequence represents how many composite numbers there are in a row before the next prime number.

Your Task

Use recursion and if/when or if-let/when-let BIFs to write a function called decode_prime_list that will decode any list passed to it as a parameter that is encoded using this interesting version of RLE. Your function must produce the list of prime numbers contained in RLEPRIMES or any similarly encoded list. The result list must be ordered from least to greatest.

Review

After completing the previous steps:

  1. Review one of the many possible solutions for this task,
  2. Compare and contrast your code with the possible solutions’ code, and
  3. Make note of any improvements you could make for future tasks.
